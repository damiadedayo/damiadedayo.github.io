Production Readiness Assessment

The prototype as built represents a proof-of-concept for the exoskeletal design and fabrication approach rather than a fully production-ready unit. To assess whether this design could theoretically become deployable, several dimensions were evaluated:

Display and containment — The prototype is intended to be housed in a display case, which avoids the majority of risks associated with its school setting. Accessible surfaces within a cased display are safe for the expected audience. Internal components should only be accessed by technical staff for maintenance, not by users.

Durability and lifespan — Further testing would be required to determine the operational lifespan of the prototype under normal usage conditions. The repeated stress caused by repeated wing motion will cause cumulative fatigue in both the structural components and the string actuation system. The frequency of maintenance required cannot be determined without extended run testing.

Repairability — The majority of components involved in actuation are designed to be replaceable without requiring destruction of the skin or skeleton. The designated access points with magnetic or screwed closures in the skin allow maintenance personnel to reach internal components. For significant structural failure of the skeletal framework, a write-off and reprint of affected components would be the most practical approach, given the low cost of PETG printing relative to extensive repair.

Scalability — The design could theoretically be scaled to a larger form factor in future. This would require appropriate stress testing of the structure at scale, and reconsideration of the actuation system. A string-pull mechanism that is adequate at one foot wingspan would require significantly more torque at larger scales, and the string itself may become a failure point.

Industrial Context: Animatronics

The application of animatronic technology in commercial and educational settings is well-established. Theme parks employ large-scale animatronic figures with pneumatic and hydraulic actuation systems capable of producing complex, lifelike movement at scales far beyond what an academic prototype can achieve. Museum installations use similar technology, often with a higher priority placed on anatomical accuracy and long operational lifetimes. The techniques explored in this module (3D printing, silicone skinning, compliant mechanism design) are all present, in more industrially refined forms, in professional animatronic pipelines.

At an industrial scale, the choice of actuation technology would shift. Servo motors are appropriate for small-scale, low-load applications but are not used in large animatronic figures, where pneumatic cylinders or DC motors with dedicated drivers provide the power density required. The skin system would similarly be manufactured at higher specification. For example, platinum-cure silicone over flexible foam cores is standard in professional animatronic and creature effects production, closely paralleling the design approach taken in this project.

Environmental Considerations

PETG is a recyclable thermoplastic, though recycling infrastructure for mixed-colour FDM waste is limited in practice. The silicone components of the skin are not readily recyclable and represent the primary end-of-life challenge for this prototype. The fabric membrane is replaceable and its disposal impact is minimal. For a longer-term deployment, the reprintability of components is an environmental advantage: worn or failed parts can be replaced individually rather than requiring the entire prototype to be discarded.

Energy consumption during fabrication — primarily from the 3D printer and any associated workshop equipment — is worth acknowledging. Printing is relatively energy-efficient compared to subtractive machining for small components, but print time across all components accumulates to a non-trivial total.

Reflection on the Development Process

Looking back across the five stages, several decisions stand out as having had the most significant impact on the outcome. The early decision to scope down from a three-joint wing to a single-joint mechanism was the right call — it made fabrication achievable within the timeframe without meaningfully compromising the visual impact of the final prototype for its intended audience.

The servo motor budget issue was the most disruptive constraint encountered. In hindsight, motor cost should have been captured more rigorously in the cost engineering phase of Design Development, with a budget line allocated and verified before the actuation design was committed. This is a project management lesson as much as a design one: assumptions about resource availability need to be validated early, not discovered during preproduction.

The skin and membrane system was not fully realised in this prototype cycle due to time constraints, but the design work for it is complete. The fabric-and-silicone composite membrane approach and the magnetic-closure access seams represent design solutions that are both technically sound and appropriate for the use case. A full production iteration of this prototype would prioritise completing the skin system and completing actuation testing with appropriate servo hardware.

The use of 3D printing as the primary fabrication method proved well-suited to the iterative nature of this development process. The ability to revise a CAD file and reprint a component within hours — rather than days, as would be the case with tooled manufacturing — made it practical to respond to fit and function issues discovered during assembly. In a commercial product development context, this flexibility is precisely why FDM prototyping occupies such a central role in early-stage development pipelines, even where the final production method will be entirely different.
