From Files to Physical Components

Preproduction marks the point at which the virtual prototype became a physical artefact. The PETG components were sent to print having been prepared and sliced in the Detailed Development stage. The transition from digital file to physical object is rarely without issue, and this build was no exception — several adjustments were required between the initial print output and the components used in assembly.

Print Modifications and Iteration

Three categories of modification were made to the print preparation settings following assessment of initial outputs:

Supports — Support structures were added to regions where overhanging geometry would otherwise have caused print failure or surface degradation on the underside of features. This was particularly relevant for the joint components, where the geometry includes overhanging surfaces that the printer cannot bridge unsupported across the required span.

Component spacing — Spacing between assembled components was reviewed and adjusted. Initial fits between mating parts were too tight, creating excessive friction that would impede the motion of the wing mechanism. Clearance gaps were increased to allow the joint to move freely without play being introduced into the structure.

Wedge features — Wedge geometry was introduced at specific interfaces to prevent components from collapsing under load during movement. Without wedge bracing, the wing arm risked folding in an uncontrolled direction under its own weight or the tension of the actuation string, particularly during the return stroke of the flap cycle.

Actuation Testing: Servo Constraint

The intended preproduction test for the wing mechanism was to couple the assembled wing arm to a servo motor and evaluate the range of motion, string routing, and structural behaviour under actuation. This test could not be completed: the budget available to the project did not extend to the purchase of appropriate servo hardware, and no alternative source could be secured within the project timeframe.

As a result, the actuation system remained untested in the physical prototype at this stage. The string routing and roller geometry were assessed manually — confirming that the path was unobstructed and that string tension could be applied — but the dynamic behaviour of the wing under powered actuation was not observed. This is a significant limitation of the preproduction phase, and its implications for the production-readiness of the prototype are discussed in Stage 5.
