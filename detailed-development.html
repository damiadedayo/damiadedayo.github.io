3. Detailed Development

Refined modelling, actuation design, and file-to-fabrication preparation

Stage 3 Dami Adedayo

3D Modelling

Detailed development translated the design decisions of Stage 2 into fully resolved 3D models ready for fabrication. The skeletal framework and joint components were modelled in CAD software, with geometry refined to account for the mechanical constraints of the actuation system and the tolerances achievable with 3D printing.

Blender was used to create simple 3D prototypes of potential roller and linkage systems for the wing mechanism. The aim was to quickly visualise how different joint placements and motion paths would behave before committing to physical fabrication.

Actuation Mechanism: String-Pulled Wing Arm

The wing actuation mechanism was refined to a string-pull system. Strings are attached to the wing arm and routed to a motor, which applies tension to extend and withdraw the wing through its range of motion. This approach was chosen for its simplicity: it requires minimal precisely-fitted moving parts, can be routed through the skeletal structure without obstructing the skin, and is easily adjustable or replaceable if a string fails.

A roller system was designed to redirect the string path through the mechanism cleanly and reduce friction at direction changes. The geometry of the roller mounts was modelled as part of the skeletal framework, keeping the actuation system integrated into the structural design rather than as an external add-on.

Actuation Motor Selection

Selecting an appropriate motor for the wing-flapping mechanism required consideration of the motion type, torque requirements, and control complexity. A servo motor was identified as the most suitable option. Unlike a standard DC motor, which produces continuous rotation, a servo can be commanded to move between defined angular positions making it well-suited to the controlled, reciprocating motion of a wing flap. Servo motors are also significantly easier to control precisely using a microcontroller such as an Arduino, compared to a DC motor that would require additional speed and direction control circuitry.

Given the approximate one-foot wingspan of the prototype, a medium-torque metal gear servo was specified such as the MG90S or equivalent. Micro servos were ruled out as insufficiently powerful: the inertia of the wing arm and membrane during flapping would likely cause a micro servo to stall or overheat under load. A metal gear train was preferred over a plastic gear servo because it provides greater load capacity and longevity under the cyclical stress of repeated actuation.

The servo motor specification was ultimately abandoned in the final build due to budget constraints. The cost of suitable servo hardware was not covered by the available project budget, and no alternative source was available within the timeframe. This is a significant gap between the designed and built states of the prototype, and is discussed further in the Preproduction and Production stages.

Material Finalisation: PETG

PETG was confirmed as the print material for all structural components during this stage. The properties that made it appropriate were its combination of toughness (it does not shatter under impact the way PLA can) good temperature resistance, strong inter-layer adhesion compared to PLA, and reliable printability without the warping issues associated with ABS. For a prototype intended for a school environment, the impact resistance of PETG is particularly relevant. Components that might be accidentally struck or dropped have a much higher chance of surviving intact in PETG than in brittle PLA.

PETG's UV resistance was also a consideration for a display piece that may be in ambient light for extended periods, and its chemical resistance provides some protection against cleaning products that might be used in a school setting.

Print Preparation

With models finalised, files were prepared for 3D printing. This stage involved orienting components to minimise support material, assessing where support structures were unavoidable, and verifying that wall thicknesses and feature sizes were within the reliable printing capabilities of the available hardware.
